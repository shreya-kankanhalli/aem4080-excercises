Where we start

A real problem, submitted anonymously

"Figuring out what to eat every single day is mentally exhausting. I'm not great at experimenting with new recipes, but I'm also tired of the same limited options. The dishes I actually enjoy take real prep work — washing, chopping, marinating — before I can even start cooking. I tried using Notion to track groceries and meals I liked, but it was too messy and I never opened it. I bookmarked recipes online but never went back to them. The whole cycle of deciding, shopping, prepping, cooking, and then eating drains the energy I need for everything else."
— AEM 4080 student · submitted this week
Reading the brief

What do we already know?

  • The core problem is decision fatigue, not cooking skill
  • The full cycle is the pain: deciding → shopping → prepping → cooking → eating
  • Notion failed — too messy, they never opened it
  • Bookmarked recipes failed — they never went back
  • Two reasonable workarounds tried — neither stuck
Your product

Meet Mise

Named after mise en place — the chef's practice of having everything ready before you start cooking.

Your team just got a $50K pre-seed check to build a mobile app that eliminates the daily meal planning cycle for college students.

You have 20 minutes. Let's build.

⏱ Round 1 · 5 minutes

Build your v1

Paste this prompt into Lovable. Don't change anything yet — just explore what generates.
"Build a mobile app called Mise that helps college students decide what to cook tonight. Show a home screen with meal suggestions based on effort level (low / medium / high), a simple grocery list view, and a meal detail page with a prep checklist."
Note what feels off, missing, or confusing — you'll use this in a moment.
Checkpoint 1 · 3 minutes

First reactions

  • What did you like about what generated?
  • Did it feel like it solved the problem?
  • What felt off or missing?
⏱ Round 2 · 5 minutes

Iterate — pick one thing to fix

Based on the discussion, choose one follow-up prompt:
Option A: "Redesign the home screen so the first thing the user sees is three meal suggestions for tonight, ranked by prep time. No browsing, no search — just three options with a 'Cook This' button." Option B: "Add a quick onboarding screen that asks: How much time do you have tonight? What's already in your fridge? Use those answers to filter suggestions." Option C: "Add a 'I made this' button on the meal detail page that logs it to a simple history so the app learns what the user actually likes over time."
Checkpoint 2 · 3 minutes

How did it change — and what's still missing?

It's 7pm. The student who submitted this just got back from class. They're tired. Walk us through your updated prototype.
  • What did you change, and what drove that decision?
  • If this person downloaded the app tonight, what would make them open it again tomorrow?
  • What's the one thing you'd want to test with a real user before building further?
After checkpoint 2

If you have time: one more iteration

Based on what came up in the discussion, write your own follow-up prompt. Try to address the biggest gap your team identified.

You don't have to use the suggested options. The best prompt will come from what you noticed about your own prototype.

When you're done, be ready to share: what did you change, and why?
